\section*{Abstract}

Predicting how mutations affect the aggregation propensity of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) is critical for understanding neurodegenerative diseases. Here we present a conditional flow matching framework that generates mutation-specific conformational ensembles from protein language model (PLM) embeddings. Evaluating on deep mutational scanning (DMS) data for amyloid-$\beta$ 42 (A$\beta$42, $n$=751) and islet amyloid polypeptide (IAPP, $n$=661), we discover a scale-dependent direction reversal: $\Delta R_g$ from ESM-2 35M correlates positively with nucleation scores ($\rho$=+0.229), while ESM-2 650M yields a negative correlation ($\rho$=$-$0.219). However, layer-wise linear probing reveals that both PLMs encode aggregation propensity in the \textit{same} positive direction ($\rho$=0.40--0.66 across all layers and scales), demonstrating that the reversal originates not in the PLM representations but in the flow model's conversion of embeddings to three-dimensional structures. Cross-scale CKA analysis shows that 650M late layers have no representational counterpart in 35M, yet both encode nucleation equally well. Combining the opposing $\Delta R_g$ signals from both scales yields $\rho$=0.349 (5-fold CV), an absolute improvement of $\Delta\rho$=0.112 over the single-scale baseline. Furthermore, supervised Ridge regression on raw ESM-2 embeddings achieves $\rho$=0.40--0.66 (depending on regularization) without any structure generation, demonstrating that PLM embeddings contain substantially more aggregation-relevant information than the flow model's $\Delta R_g$ captures. These findings reveal that generative structure models can systematically distort PLM-encoded biological signals and caution against interpreting structural predictions as faithful reflections of the underlying sequence representations.

\section*{Introduction}

Intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) play central roles in neurodegenerative diseases through aberrant aggregation. Amyloid-$\beta$ 42 (A$\beta$42) aggregates into plaques in Alzheimer's disease, while islet amyloid polypeptide (IAPP) forms toxic deposits in type 2 diabetes. Understanding how mutations modulate aggregation propensity is essential for interpreting variants of uncertain significance (VUS) and guiding therapeutic development.

Deep mutational scanning (DMS) has generated comprehensive maps of mutational effects on amyloid nucleation for both A$\beta$42 \cite{seuma2022} ($n$=751 single-point substitutions) and IAPP \cite{badia2026} ($n$=661). These datasets provide ground truth for benchmarking computational predictors but remain limited to specific proteins and experimental conditions.

Current computational approaches for predicting mutation effects on IDP aggregation fall into two categories. Sequence-based methods, including ESM-2 log-likelihood ratios (LLR) \cite{lin2023} and the recently published CANYA neural network \cite{thompson2025}, predict aggregation propensity directly from amino acid sequence. These methods are fast but cannot capture the conformational dynamics that underlie aggregation. Structure-based methods, primarily molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, can model mutation-specific conformational changes but are computationally prohibitive for genome-scale variant interpretation.

Flow matching generative models have recently emerged as powerful tools for protein conformational ensemble generation. AlphaFlow \cite{jing2024} fine-tunes AlphaFold under a flow matching objective, while P2DFlow \cite{p2dflow2025} introduces SE(3)-equivariant flow matching for ensembles. IDPFold2 \cite{idpfold2_2026} integrates mixture-of-experts into flow matching for IDP-specific ensemble prediction. However, none of these methods have been applied to predict mutation effects on IDP aggregation.

Here we present a conditional flow matching framework that generates mutation-specific conformational ensembles by conditioning on ESM-2 protein language model embeddings. We make four contributions: (1) we discover that the direction of the $\Delta R_g$--nucleation correlation reverses depending on PLM scale, and show through layer-wise probing and CKA analysis that this reversal originates in the flow model's learned mapping, not in the PLM representations themselves; (2) we demonstrate that combining opposing $\Delta R_g$ signals from two scales yields $\rho$=0.349 ($\Delta\rho$=+0.112 over single-scale); (3) we show that supervised probes on raw ESM-2 embeddings achieve $\rho$=0.40--0.66 (depending on regularization), substantially exceeding the unsupervised $\Delta R_g$ ($\rho$=0.23) and revealing that structure generation discards linearly decodable aggregation information; and (4) we validate on two independent amyloid DMS datasets (A$\beta$42 and IAPP).

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Flow matching generates mutation-specific conformational ensembles}

We trained a conditional flow matching model (MutationConditionedStructureHead) that takes ESM-2 sequence embeddings, noised C$\alpha$ coordinates, and mutation position/identity as input, and outputs a velocity field for ODE integration. Starting from Gaussian noise, 50-step Euler integration produces C$\alpha$ coordinate ensembles of 32--64 structures per mutation. The model was trained on conformational ensembles from the Protein Ensemble Database (PED) for three IDP targets: tau K18 (PED00017), $\alpha$-synuclein (PED00024), and A$\beta$42 (PED00531).

\subsection*{$\Delta R_g$ predicts amyloid nucleation scores}

We scored all 751 single-point substitutions from the Seuma et al. A$\beta$42 DMS dataset \cite{seuma2022} by generating mutation-specific ensembles and computing $\Delta R_g = R_g^{\text{mut}} - R_g^{\text{WT}}$. Using ESM-2 35M embeddings, $\Delta R_g$ showed a significant positive correlation with experimental nucleation scores ($\rho$=0.229, $p$=1.4$\times$10$^{-9}$, 95\% CI [0.148, 0.285]; Fig~\ref{fig:scatter}a). This correlation survived Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons across 10 tests (adjusted $p$=7.1$\times$10$^{-9}$).

Among seven trajectory-derived features tested, $\Delta R_g$ was the strongest individual predictor and the most important feature in both Lasso regression (weight=0.298) and Random Forest (Gini importance=0.338) models. Other trajectory features showed no significant correlation after multiple comparison correction (Fig~\ref{fig:forest}).

Importantly, $\Delta R_g$ and LLR capture partially independent information: their cross-correlation was $\rho$=0.369, indicating that approximately 86\% of the variance in $\Delta R_g$ is not explained by LLR. A composite model combining all seven features achieved a cross-validated Spearman $\rho$ of 0.285 (Gradient Boosting, 5-fold CV), confirming the additive value of structural features.

\subsection*{ESM-2 scale determines correlation direction}

When we repeated the analysis using ESM-2 650M embeddings, the $\Delta R_g$--nucleation correlation reversed direction: $\rho$=$-$0.219 ($p$=4.0$\times$10$^{-10}$; Fig~\ref{fig:scatter}b). The negative correlation indicates that mutations causing compaction are associated with increased nucleation, consistent with the established biophysical mechanism for A$\beta$42 aggregation.

To determine whether this reversal originates from the PLM representations or the flow model architecture, we conducted a controlled experiment with identical architecture ($d_{\text{model}}$=256, $n_{\text{heads}}$=8, $n_{\text{layers}}$=6) but different ESM-2 embeddings (Table~\ref{tab:controlled}; Fig~\ref{fig:controlled}):

The direction reversal occurred only with ESM-2 650M, ruling out flow model architecture (width, depth) as the cause. This narrows the origin to the interaction between PLM scale and the flow model's learned mapping. Crucially, as we show below, the PLM embeddings themselves do not invert the aggregation signal---the reversal arises specifically in how the flow model converts different-scale embeddings into three-dimensional structures.

\subsection*{Cross-scale combination yields 47\% improvement}

Because the 35M and 650M $\Delta R_g$ signals are anticorrelated yet both significantly predict nucleation, they carry complementary information. We combined both $\Delta R_g$ values along with their product (interaction term) and absolute difference (divergence) as features in a Gradient Boosting model with 5-fold cross-validation:

The best model (LLR + cross-scale full, Gradient Boosting) achieved $\rho$=0.349, a 47\% improvement over the single-scale baseline ($\rho$=0.237). This confirms that the direction reversal contains exploitable complementary information.

\subsection*{Reversal originates in the flow model, not PLM embeddings}

To determine whether the direction reversal reflects a genuine difference in how PLMs encode aggregation, we trained Ridge regression probes directly from per-layer ESM-2 representations to nucleation scores, bypassing the flow model entirely.

Strikingly, both 35M and 650M embeddings predicted nucleation scores in the \textit{same positive direction} at every layer (Fig~\ref{fig:layerrho}). ESM-2 35M Layer 1 achieved $\rho$=0.660, and 650M Layer 2 achieved $\rho$=0.622. Even with PCA reduction to 10 components (eliminating overfitting concerns), both scales maintained strong positive correlations (35M: $\rho$=0.396; 650M: $\rho$=0.426).

This demonstrates that the scale-dependent direction reversal does not originate in the PLM representations. Both models encode aggregation propensity equally well and in the same direction. The reversal arises from the flow model's conversion of embeddings to three-dimensional coordinates, where the mapping from embedding space to structural space ($\Delta R_g$) differs qualitatively between scales.

\subsection*{CKA analysis reveals representational divergence without directional change}

To understand the relationship between 35M and 650M representations, we computed linear CKA (Centered Kernel Alignment \cite{kornblith2019}) across all layer pairs (Fig~\ref{fig:cka}). The CKA matrix reveals that 650M early layers (0--10) are representationally similar to 35M mid layers (2--6; CKA$>$0.6), while 650M late layers (15+) have no counterpart in 35M (CKA$<$0.2). Despite this structural divergence, both encode nucleation scores with comparable accuracy---the 650M-specific late-layer representations do not invert the biological signal; they simply encode it through a different representational geometry that the flow model interprets differently.

\subsection*{Supervised embedding probes reveal an information gap}

To quantify how much aggregation-relevant information the PLM embeddings contain beyond what $\Delta R_g$ captures, we trained supervised Ridge regression probes on nucleation score labels (5-fold CV). ESM-2 35M Layer 1 mean-pooled embeddings achieved $\rho$=0.660. Under conservative PCA-10 regularization (eliminating overfitting concerns at 10 features / 751 samples), the probe still achieved $\rho$=0.396. Both substantially exceed the unsupervised flow model $\Delta R_g$ ($\rho$=0.229), though we emphasize these are not directly comparable: the probe has supervised access to nucleation scores, while $\Delta R_g$ is a label-free structural metric.

The flow model compresses rich 480/1280-dimensional PLM representations into a single scalar ($\Delta R_g$). While supervised probes and unsupervised $\Delta R_g$ are not directly comparable, the magnitude of the gap ($\rho$=0.40 vs. 0.23 under the most conservative comparison) indicates that substantial aggregation-relevant information is lost in the structure generation step. The flow model provides interpretable structural ensembles, but the $\Delta R_g$ summary statistic is a lossy compression of the underlying biological signal.

\subsection*{Per-residue analysis reveals scale-dependent structural responses}

To understand the mechanistic basis of the direction reversal, we analyzed per-residue contributions to $\Delta R_g$ for familial Alzheimer's disease (fAD) mutations (Fig~\ref{fig:perresidue}). The 35M model showed large $\Delta R_g$ contributions at the C-terminus (residues 35--42), far from the mutation sites, suggesting non-local and potentially artifactual structural responses. In contrast, the 650M model showed $\Delta R_g$ contributions concentrated near the mutation site, consistent with local structural perturbation expected from single-point mutations.

\subsection*{Multi-amyloid validation on IAPP}

To test generalization beyond the training distribution, we scored the Badia et al. IAPP DMS dataset \cite{badia2026} ($n$=661 single-point substitutions). IAPP was not included in the PED training data, making this an out-of-distribution test. $\Delta R_g$ from the 35M flow model showed a significant correlation with IAPP nucleation scores ($\rho$=0.110, $p$=1.3$\times$10$^{-3}$, BH-adjusted $p$=3.1$\times$10$^{-3}$; Fig~\ref{fig:multiamyloid}). Cross-scale combination yielded only marginal improvement for IAPP (best LR CV $\rho$=0.121), consistent with the 650M flow model producing near-zero signal for this out-of-distribution protein ($\rho$=0.038).

We also observed a direction asymmetry: $\Delta R_g$ correlates more strongly with slowing mutations ($\rho$=0.172, $p$=1.6$\times$10$^{-4}$) than accelerating mutations ($\rho$=0.096, NS), consistent with findings from Badia et al. \cite{badia2026}.

\subsection*{Comparison with existing methods}

Flow $\Delta R_g$ (35M) outperformed the recently published CANYA aggregation predictor \cite{thompson2025} on A$\beta$42 DMS ($\rho$=0.229 vs. 0.115; Fig~\ref{fig:comparison}). While CANYA is a general-purpose predictor not specifically designed for A$\beta$42, this comparison demonstrates that mutation-specific structural ensemble information provides stronger predictive signal than sequence features alone.

\section*{Discussion}

\subsection*{Structure generation as information bottleneck}

Our central finding is that generative structure models can systematically distort the biological signals encoded in protein language model embeddings. ESM-2 embeddings at both 35M and 650M scales encode aggregation propensity in the same positive direction ($\rho$=0.40--0.66), yet the flow model converts these into $\Delta R_g$ values with opposite signs. This means the widely observed ``scaling effect'' in our structural predictions is not a property of the PLM representations but an artifact of how the structure generation model interprets them.

The information loss is substantial: a supervised Ridge probe on 480-dimensional ESM-2 embeddings achieves $\rho$=0.40 (PCA-10, conservative) to $\rho$=0.66 (full features), while the unsupervised $\Delta R_g$ scalar achieves $\rho$=0.23. Although these operate in different regimes---the probe is supervised on nucleation score labels while $\Delta R_g$ requires no labels---the gap demonstrates that the flow model's structural bottleneck discards aggregation-relevant information that is linearly decodable from the embeddings. This has practical implications for the growing number of methods that chain PLM embeddings through generative structure models.

\subsection*{Why does the flow model invert the 650M signal?}

We propose that the reversal reflects a fundamental difference in how the flow model maps embedding geometry to physical structure. The 35M flow model (trained with 480-dim input) learns to associate aggregation-promoting mutations with extended conformations ($\Delta R_g > 0$), while the 650M model (trained with 1280-dim input) associates the same mutations with compact conformations ($\Delta R_g < 0$). CKA analysis shows that 650M late layers (15+) have no representational counterpart in 35M, suggesting the 650M model uses a qualitatively different geometric mapping for structure generation.

Notably, the 650M direction (compact $\rightarrow$ aggregation) aligns with the established biophysical mechanism for A$\beta$42, where hydrophobic core collapse nucleates fibril formation. The 35M direction (extended $\rightarrow$ aggregation) may reflect exposure of aggregation-prone regions. Both are physically plausible mechanisms, but the flow model can only represent one per scale.

\subsection*{Implications for PLM-based prediction}

Our results suggest that for mutation effect prediction, raw PLM embeddings with simple linear models may outperform elaborate generative pipelines. The Ridge probe achieves $\rho$=0.60 with no structure generation, no GPU-intensive sampling, and no training of additional models. This aligns with recent observations that medium-sized PLMs perform well at transfer learning \cite{medium_plm2025} and that only 39\% of protein fitness tasks show predictable scaling behavior \cite{scaling2025}.

However, structure generation provides something that embedding probes cannot: interpretable three-dimensional ensembles. The per-residue $\Delta R_g$ analysis (Fig~\ref{fig:perresidue}) reveals spatially resolved structural responses to mutations, which is valuable for mechanistic understanding even if the aggregate $\Delta R_g$ scalar is a lossy summary.

\subsection*{Limitations}

\textbf{In-distribution bias.} A$\beta$42 conformational ensembles from PED were included in the training data. The higher A$\beta$42 flow model performance ($\rho$=0.229) relative to out-of-distribution IAPP ($\rho$=0.110) likely reflects this bias. Cross-scale combination did not improve IAPP predictions, consistent with the 650M flow model producing near-zero signal for out-of-distribution proteins.

\textbf{Embedding probe generalization.} The $\rho$=0.66 Ridge probe result is for A$\beta$42 only. Whether raw ESM-2 embeddings generalize to other amyloid systems with comparable accuracy remains to be tested. The high dimensionality of the probe (480 features, 751 samples) warrants caution, though PCA-10 controls ($\rho$=0.40) confirm the signal is robust.

\textbf{Unphysical absolute structures.} While relative $\Delta R_g$ rank ordering is informative, the controlled-experiment models produced unphysical structures ($R_g > 100$~\AA{}). Absolute structures should not be interpreted as physical conformations.

\textbf{Cross-protein transfer failure.} Transfer from A$\beta$42 DMS to tau/$\alpha$-synuclein disease mutations was not successful, consistent with protein-specific aggregation mechanisms \cite{badia2026}.

\section*{Materials and Methods}

\subsection*{Conditional flow matching model}

The MutationConditionedStructureHead is a Transformer-based velocity network with 6 layers, 256-dimensional hidden states, and 8 attention heads. Input features include ESM-2 sequence embeddings (projected to $d_{\text{model}}$), noised C$\alpha$ coordinates encoded as RBF distance features (16 bins, max 20~\AA), and mutation position/amino acid embeddings (32-dimensional). The model is trained with the conditional flow matching objective \cite{lipman2023} using AdamW optimizer, cosine learning rate schedule, and EMA (decay 0.999).

\subsection*{ESM-2 embeddings}

Three ESM-2 model scales were used: esm2\_t12\_35M\_UR50D (480-dim), esm2\_t30\_150M\_UR50D (640-dim), and esm2\_t33\_650M\_UR50D (1280-dim) \cite{lin2023}. For the controlled experiment, all embedding dimensions were projected to $d_{\text{model}}$=256 via a learned linear layer.

\subsection*{DMS datasets}

A$\beta$42 nucleation scores for 751 substitutions from Seuma et al. \cite{seuma2022}. IAPP nucleation scores for 661 substitutions from Badia et al. \cite{badia2026}, processed from the singles.df table ($nscore\_c$ column).

\subsection*{Layer-wise embedding analysis}

To determine whether the direction reversal originates in PLM representations or the flow model, we extracted mean-pooled representations from every transformer layer of ESM-2 35M (13 layers) and 650M (34 layers) for all 751 A$\beta$42 mutant sequences. For each layer, a Ridge regression probe ($\alpha$=1.0) was trained with 5-fold cross-validation to predict nucleation scores directly from the layer's representation, with StandardScaler fitted within each fold to prevent data leakage. PCA-reduced variants (10, 30, 50, 100 components) served as overfitting controls.

Cross-scale representational similarity was quantified using linear CKA (Centered Kernel Alignment) \cite{kornblith2019}, computed on the Gram matrices $K = XX^T$ and $L = YY^T$ to handle different embedding dimensions across scales.

\subsection*{Statistical analysis}

Spearman rank correlation for all analyses. Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals from 10,000 resamples. Benjamini-Hochberg correction across all tested features. Cross-validated performance with 5-fold CV using Gradient Boosting, Lasso regression, and Ridge regression.
